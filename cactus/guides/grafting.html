What is Grafting?

Grafting joins the vascular tissue of a scion (the plant you want to grow) onto a rootstock (a vigorous root system). The rootstock provides water and nutrients; the scion grows faster than it would on its own roots.

Why graft? Speed up slow growers (Ariocarpus, Aztekium), save rotting plants by grafting healthy tissue above the rot line, propagate chlorophyll-deficient mutants (moon cactus), or grow multiple varieties on one rootstock.

Step-by-Step Grafting Process

1️⃣ Sterilize Everything

Clean your blade (razor blade or sharp grafting knife) with isopropyl alcohol. Dip in sulfur powder between cuts. Work in a clean, low-dust environment.

2️⃣ Prepare the Rootstock

Cut the rootstock at the desired height with a single, clean, flat horizontal cut. A jagged or angled cut reduces contact area and lowers success rate. Remove the top and discard (or root it for another plant). Bevel the edge slightly — this helps the scion sit flush and prevents water pooling.

3️⃣ Prepare the Scion

Cut the bottom of the scion with a matching flat cut. The cut surfaces must be identical in shape and size for maximum vascular contact. Remove any spines from the bottom section of the scion that would touch the rootstock.

4️⃣ Align Vascular Rings

This is the most critical step. The vascular ring (cambium) is visible as a lighter ring under the green skin — it's where water and nutrients flow. At least part of the scion's vascular ring must overlap with the rootstock's ring. Off-center alignment = failed graft. Rotate the scion to maximize overlap.

5️⃣ Press & Secure

Press the scion firmly onto the rootstock — no air gaps. Secure with:

  • Grafting bands: Stretchy rubber or silicone bands. Apply even pressure from top to bottom.
  • Parafilm: Wraps around the joint. Keeps moisture in. Stretches as the graft heals.
  • Pantihose/stockings: Old-school method. Works great. Stretchy, breathable.
  • Weight method: Place a small weight on top of the scion (for flat grafts).

Pressure matters: Enough to hold firm contact, but not so tight you crush tissue. The scion should not be able to slide or rotate.

6️⃣ Healing Environment

Place the graft in a warm (22-28°C), bright location with no direct sun for 2-4 weeks. High humidity helps. Don't water for the first week — moisture at the graft union causes rot.

7️⃣ Aftercare

After 2-4 weeks, gently check if the scion is firmly attached. If it wiggles, re-wrap and wait longer. Once healed, remove bands. The scion will now grow at the rootstock's speed. Pups may appear on the rootstock below the graft — remove them immediately or they'll steal energy from the scion.

Degrafting

When the scion has grown large enough (6-12 months), you can remove it from the rootstock and root it independently. This is called degrafting. Cut the scion off the rootstock with ~2-3cm of base attached. Let it callous for 2 weeks, then root in dry pumice. The original rootstock can then host a new scion.

When Not to Graft

  • Fast-growing species don't need it — T. pachanoi, T. bridgesii grow fine on their own roots
  • Winter — Grafting during winter dormancy has very low success rates
  • Unhealthy plants — Both rootstock and scion should be actively growing and pest-free
  • No clear reason — Grafting has risks. Only do it when there's a clear benefit (speed, saving a plant, propagation)
