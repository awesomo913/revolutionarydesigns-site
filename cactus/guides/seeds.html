1. Getting Seeds

You have three options for obtaining cactus seeds:

🌸 From Your Own Plants

Cross-pollinate two different clones of the same species (or close hybrids). Use a clean paintbrush to transfer pollen from one flower's anthers to another's stigma. Wait 2-6 months for the fruit to ripen.

🛒 Trusted Suppliers

Specialist cactus seed vendors with reliable germination rates: Mesa Garden (New Mexico — massive Trichocereus selection), Koehres Kakteen (Germany — European specialists), Precious Cactus (Italy — excellent for rare species).

🤝 Community Trades

Join Facebook groups like SOLO CACTUS, Grafting Cacti, or Trichocereus Auctions. Many growers trade fresh seed. Accession codes (e.g. EF15A27/EFC1655) track provenance and are highly valued.

2. Seed Extraction & Cleaning

Left: ripe fruit → Cut open to reveal seeds in pulp → Cleaned, dry seed ready for sowing

Cleaning Process

  • Trichocereus fruit: Split open, scoop out the pink/white pulp containing tiny black seeds. Place in a fine sieve and rinse under running water, massaging gently to separate seeds from pulp. Spread on a paper towel and air-dry for 3-5 days.
  • Lophophora fruit: Small pink berries. Squeeze onto a plate. The seeds are larger and easier to separate. Rinse and dry.
  • Echinocactus fruit: Dry, woolly capsules. Crush open and shake out seeds. No rinsing needed.

3. Sterilization (Optional but Recommended)

Seeds from outdoor plants may carry fungal spores that kill seedlings. Sterilization reduces damping-off (seedling wilt).

  • Hydrogen peroxide soak: 3% H₂O₂ for 10 minutes → rinse with sterile water
  • Hot water treatment: 50°C water for 15-20 minutes (careful — higher temps kill seeds)
  • Fungicide: Light dusting with sulfur powder or copper fungicide

4. Sowing — The Takeaway Method

The most reliable method for Trichocereus and most cacti is the takeaway tek — a sealed container that maintains high humidity for the first 6-12 months.

Takeaway container setup: clear lid, sterile 50/50 soil, seeds on surface

Step-by-Step Sowing

1️⃣ Prepare Container

Use a clear takeaway container or seedling tray with a transparent lid. Clean with 10% bleach solution. Drill 3-4 small drainage holes in the bottom.

2️⃣ Add Substrate

Fill with a sterile mix: 50% fine pumice + 50% coir (or fine sand). Moisten with distilled water — it should be damp but not wet. Microwave the damp mix for 2 minutes to sterilize, then cool completely.

3️⃣ Sow Seeds

Scatter seeds evenly on the surface. Do not bury them — cactus seeds need light to germinate. Press gently to ensure contact with the soil. Mist lightly with distilled water.

4️⃣ Seal & Label

Close the lid. Label with species, date, and source code. Place in bright indirect light (no direct sun — the sealed container will overheat).

5. Germination Care

First 3 Months

  • Humidity: Keep sealed. The lid should be foggy. Open once a week for 10 minutes to exchange air.
  • Light: Bright indirect (12-14 hours/day). No direct sun through the sealed container — it acts as a greenhouse and will cook the seedlings.
  • Temperature: 22-28°C constant. A heat mat helps for stubborn species (Lophophora, Ariocarpus).
  • Water: Don't water — the sealed container maintains moisture. If the soil looks dry, mist with distilled water.
⚠️ Damping-off: If seedlings suddenly collapse at the base, it's a fungal infection. Remove affected seedlings immediately. Improve air circulation. Next time, sterilize your soil.

Months 3-6

  • Start opening the lid more frequently over 2-3 weeks to harden off (acclimate to lower humidity).
  • Begin very dilute fertilizer (¼ strength, high-nitrogen, once a month).
  • Seedlings should be 5-15mm tall with visible spines.

6. First Potting (6-12 Months)

When seedlings are large enough to handle (5-10mm), transplant into individual small pots (5cm / 2 inch).

  • Soil: Move to a grittier mix — 60% pumice + 40% coir. No fertilizer for 2 weeks after transplant.
  • Water: Let the soil dry between waterings. Reduce humidity gradually.
  • Light: Increase to morning sun (1-2 hours). Watch for reddening (sun stress — cut back).
  • Grafting option: At this size, you can micro-graft onto Pereskiopsis for 10x growth rate. See the grafting guide.

8. Why Graft?

Grafting 6-month-old seedlings onto Pereskiopsis rootstock can turn a 3-year growth timeline into 6 months. The scion grows 5-10x faster on a vigorous rootstock. Once the scion reaches reasonable size, you degraft it onto its own roots or re-graft onto a permanent Trichocereus stock.
